I Built a Migraine App During 30 Years of Pain (and a War)

The First Attack

I was 12 years old when migraine found me.

I didn't understand what was happening. The aura came first‚Äîvisual distortions that made the world look broken. Then the pain. I remember lying in bed for hours, sleeping, scared. My parents were scared too. We didn't have a name for it yet.

In those early years, I felt defective. Different. Wrong somehow. The worst part wasn't the pain‚Äîit was the unpredictability. Migraine could arrive during a school lesson, in the middle of a normal day, turning me into "the kid who has to leave."

I didn't know if it would pass. I didn't know if it was treatable. I just knew it was crushing me.

The Hardest Years

University was brutal.

I was studying in a different city, living alone. Sometimes attacks happened during lectures. I'd have to leave, fumbling my way to public transport‚Äîsometimes with aura, literally feeling my way through buses and streets, half-blind with pain.

Those were the years I felt most broken.

But something shifted around age 35. I started training regularly. Watching my diet more carefully. Learning‚Äîslowly, painfully‚Äîthat I needed to take care of myself. Not just manage symptoms, but actually care for myself. Love myself, even.

The results were undeniable: from 15-20 migraine attacks per month down to 3-4.

Not through a miracle drug. Not through some expensive treatment. Through care. Through understanding my triggers. Through respecting my body's limits. Through sleep, nutrition, stress management, consistent exercise.

Through everything I'm now trying to teach through Migraine Companion.

I'm 42 now. I've been living with migraine for 30 years. And I'm still learning.

The Accidental Developer

April 2024. Ukraine. War.

I was bored. I needed something to occupy my mind while air raid sirens became background noise and power outages stretched to 10-16 hours a day.

I decided to learn web development. Just for fun. It worked.

Then I tried making an app‚Äînot about migraine, just to see if I could. It worked too.

Then I thought: why not make something I'd actually use? A migraine tracker for myself.

But here's the thing‚ÄîI knew I wouldn't stick with a simple tracker. I'd tried tracking before and always quit. It was too boring, too clinical. Just numbers and dates on a screen.

So I added something different: therapeutic games. Breathing Garden (4-7-8 technique). Migraine Monster (a gentle care game where you help Mi during difficult moments).

While developing Migraine Monster, I stumbled onto narrative therapy‚Äîspecifically Michael White's work on externalization.

And everything clicked.

Mi Is Not Your Enemy

What if migraine isn't the villain?

Narrative therapy suggests something radical: externalize the problem. Separate the person from the condition. You're not "a migraine sufferer"‚Äîyou're a person who lives with migraine.

In the app, this became Mi‚Äîa companion, not an enemy. The name comes from the first two letters of "Migraine" in Ukrainian (–ú—ñ–≥—Ä–µ–Ω—å). Simple, but it means everything.

Mi reflects your state. When you're calm, Mi is calm. When a storm approaches, Mi is restless. You don't fight Mi‚Äîyou understand Mi. You don't defeat Mi‚Äîyou care for Mi.

And here's what surprised me: caring for Mi teaches you to care for yourself.

The app became more than a tracker. It became a tool for learning self-love through the lens of companionship. Every time you log a meal, track sleep, practice breathing‚Äîyou're not just collecting data. You're showing Mi (and yourself) that you matter. That your wellbeing matters.

Building in the Dark

Literally.

Power outages: 10-16 hours a day. Sometimes more. I worked whenever electricity came back‚Äî7, 8, 9 hours straight while the grid was live. My wife Marina (who's brilliant with colors and themes) would guide the design direction. We'd debate character expressions, color palettes, therapeutic flows‚Äîall between blackouts.

Did I want to quit? Many times.

But I couldn't. I don't know how to quit. Never have. Even when nothing was working, when code wouldn't compile, when the whole thing felt impossible‚ÄîI kept going.

Maybe it's the same stubbornness that got me through 30 years of migraine. You don't get a choice, so you learn not to stop.

Three and a half months of development. Most of it in the dark‚Äîboth literally and metaphorically.

Then one day, I looked at the finished app with its new theme‚Äîthe Mystic Garden, the soft purples and golden lights, Mi's gentle face‚Äîand I felt something I hadn't felt in a long time.

I made something good. Something beautiful. Something warm.

And I still can't quite believe it: wow, I actually did this.

What I Want You to Know

If you don't have migraine, here's what I need you to understand:

We need to accept that there's no reliable cure. Migraine is part of our life. That's not defeatist‚Äîit's realistic. And acceptance is the first step toward actually living well.

We need to learn self-care, not just pop pills. People look for easy solutions‚Äîtake medication and move on. But that's a trap. Medication overuse is real, and it makes things worse. Real management means learning what triggers you, respecting your body's limits, and making changes.

We need to love ourselves, not hate our condition. This is the hardest part.

A Message to 12-Year-Old Me

If I could go back to that scared kid lying in bed with his first migraine, I'd say this:

You're not defective. You're not broken. You're not less than other people. You're special. Different, yes‚Äîbut special.

Learn about this condition. Study it. Respect it. Avoid what provokes it. Stop overthinking. Stop being so hard on yourself. Take care of your health. Not someday‚Äînow.

You're going to build something that helps people. You're going to turn 30 years of pain into something meaningful.

But first, you need to learn to love yourself.

What Success Looks Like

In one year? In five years?

I want Migraine Companion to be the tool that teaches people with migraine how to care for themselves. How to love themselves.

But personally? Success means I'm doing work I love. That I'm growing as a person despite chronic illness. That I'm living a full life‚Äînot a limited one‚Äîand helping others do the same.

If my journey from 15-20 attacks per month to 3-4 can help even one person, it was worth it.

I don't want to just survive migraine. I want to build beautiful things while living with it. I want to prove that chronic pain doesn't mean chronic limitation.

Migraine Companion launched last week on iOS. Android is coming this week. The website is live.

It's free. Because I remember what it's like to search for help and find only expensive subscriptions.

It's built on narrative therapy, evidence-based techniques, and 30 years of lived experience. It tracks 43 factors, predicts attacks 24 hours in advance, and monitors medication safety based on WHO guidelines.

But more than anything, it's built to help you do what took me 30 years to learn:

Accept. Care. Love.

Not the migraine. Yourself.
