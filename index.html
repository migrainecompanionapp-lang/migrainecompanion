More than predictions · the philosophy

Mi doesn't fight your migraine.
Mi keeps you company through it.

Most apps — and most of us — treat migraine as an enemy to battle. But the research on chronic illness points the other way: combat language (“fighting,” “battling,” “victim of”) deepens helplessness and turns up the body's stress response. A companion frame does the opposite.

You are not your migraine.

Mi is built on narrative therapy — the approach of Michael White and David Epston. Its core move is externalization: separating the person from the problem. You're not “a migraineur.” You're someone who experiences migraine — and that small shift in language loosens the grip, lowers self-blame, and gives back a sense of agency. Mi is that idea made into a character: not an enemy to defeat, but a companion whose calm reflects your own care.

Around that philosophy, Mi gives you tools for the hard moments:

What we won't pretend

Mi can be wrong — it misses some attacks and flags some quiet days. Its accuracy is still being validated, and it learns from every migraine you log. We'd rather tell you that plainly than sell certainty we haven't earned. The prodrome science is settled; that your personal engine is accurate enough is what we're proving now.

Why this exists

Thirty years of migraine.
Built into an app, during a war.

My first migraine came at twelve, with aura. For thirty years it ran my life — up to fifteen or twenty attacks a month. Slowly, by tracking and learning my own patterns, I brought that down to three or four.

I build MIgraine Companion from Dnipro, through the war — some nights writing code by candlelight when the power is out. I'm patient zero: every prediction is tested on my own body first. My wife and co-founder Marina leads the design — she made it photophobia-first, because she's watched what light does to me during an attack.

Just the two of us. Thirty years of pain turned into something that might spare someone else a little of it.
